RE:Play, RE:Experience, RE:Cycle

Welcome to RE:

The 'word' "RE:", shorthand for redo, or simply the prefix is a string of symbols about looking back. One will typically see it used for game releases or remasters.

RE: is an information centre about all kinds of homebrew and mods for tech of the past. That DS sitting in your drawer, or your childhood PlayStation can be turned into devices with impressive capabilities via some tinkering. Nowadays, tech is all about excitement and pushing limits, but I'm sure you have fond memories of devices with less power than the average smartphone. Sometimes slowing down, and looking back, is the best way forward.

As somebody who only got as far as they have thanks to curiousity (and maybe some luck), I believe one of the most important parts of my journey into the tech industry was through learning about homebrew software, and custom firmware. What does that mean? Read on to find out all you could want to know and then some!

The medium of games is one of the most powerful there is, allowing each player to experience a story crafted by somebody firsthand. Proper maintainence and modification to your devices can allow them to last for many more years to come, preserving an often overlooked part of history.

Unsure where to start?

Hacking and homebrew is a vast topic, and there are so many avenues you are able to go down. The best thing to do is look through your own old devices and go from there! The information page has all you could need to get started learning about how you can hack your very own system.

Is homebrew allowed?

The short answer is yes. Homebrewing your consoles and devices is a bit of a legal gray area, especially with newer consoles such as the PlayStation 5, pirates will make the effort to exploit security vulnerabilities in order to play free games. Of course, when a device is no longer in circulation there would be no forseeable harm in the upkeep and extending of the lifespan of your device. Ultimately it is a complex topic that requires some nuance. But I believe that preserving old game consoles is just as important as keeping things like records around, classics will always be classics, after all.
